WHAT IS JOB EVALUATION?
Job evaluation (JE) is a process designed to establish the relative internal value of each position at COMPANIA DEMO. All positions at COMPANIA DEMO make different contributions to the success of our company in different ways. These contributions are influenced by the position's Key Result Areas (KRAs), skills, experience and the position's responsibilities. JE analyzes the skills, experience, and responsibilities to determine a position's relative internal value within COMPANIA DEMO. Positions with higher skills and whose contributions to the organization may be critical will be valued higher than positions that have less skill requirements and less critical contributions.
WHY DO JOB EVALUATION?
JE establishes a consistent and easy way to understand how to measure a position's internal worth. The internal worth for each job determines the job's level in an organization. The position level is the basis for determining what the salary level should be for the position. The salary level is also influenced by COMPANIA DEMO's compensation philosophy and the external market place.
HOW DOES JOB EVALUATION WORK?
JE is more meaningful when a position is divided into parts. These parts are called compensable factors. Each of these factors reflects which COMPANIA DEMO values are important for each position and also reflects our values as a company. These compensable factors are assigned points and the total of these points will establish the overall ranking of a position. The ranking or level determines a position's relative worth within COMPANIA DEMO.
JE and its compensable factors are designed to establish (1) fairness and consistency in determining the internal worth of one job to an other and (2) the basis upon which salary is administered.
JE serves as measuring stick against which to measure and compare the value of positions on the basis of a common denominator. The COMPANIA DEMO system uses points as the common denominator.
The process of determining the relative value of all positions at COMPANIA DEMO is subjective to some degree. However, by assigning points to positions, the process becomes more objective and fair. The process is not a scientific one, but can be expected to be reasonably consistent, accurate and flexible.

COMPENSABLE FACTORS AND COMPANIA DEMO BUSINESS VALUES The JE Plan covers all positions using six compensable factors. Many of these existed in the previous JE Plan. COMPANIA DEMO believes these are critical factors that are important to our success and can be found in every job in varying degrees.
COMPANIA DEMO COMPENSABLE FACTORS 1. Knowledge 2. Communications 3. Problem Solving 4. Decision Making 5. Business Impact 6. Working Conditions
Each of the six compensable factors in the JE Plan are further defined into subfactors that represent an increasing level of value to COMPANIA DEMO. Each of these sub-factors have points. This point factor approach requires the user to assign a sub-factor for each compensable factor which best describes the position requirements.
WHAT IS UNIQUE ABOUT THE COMPANIA DEMO JOB EVALUATION PLAN? COMPANIA DEMO has revised its current JE Plan so that it reflects our current business needs and our core business values. Just as COMPANIA DEMO needs to change business strategies to meet new business needs, its internal systems must also change. The revised COMPANIA DEMO JE Plan reflects our company's changing business needs. In the Appendix, these values and the JE plan will be discussed in further detail.

POSITION TITLE: Fleet Manager REPORTS TO: Director, Administration STAFF REPORTING TO: · Drivers (37) · Fleet Coordinator (2) · Admin. Assistant

DEPARTMENT: Administration DATE: May 22nd 1998

PURPOSE OF POSITION: Oversees the operation and maintenance of company owned and leased motor vehicles and ensures that effective transportation arrangements for employees are in place.

QUALi FICATIONS: Degree in business or equivalent. Three years in the transportation industry.

PRIMARY RESPONSIBILITIES:
· Ensures that all staff are fully trained in department policies and procedures, that they have established KRA's and objectives in place, and that they are motivated and performing the established standards. This requires the employee to set staff objectives, review their performance and where required, take corrective action to deal with below standard performance. When warranted, the employee makes salary recommendations for the staff.

· Develops the department's annual budget and monitors the results on a monthly basis to assess any variances. Negative variances are investigated and explained to his/her manager and a process is developed to prevent future negative variances.

· Directs the planning, coordinating and evaluation of the department's overall results on a weekly basis and takes corrective action within departmental guidelines when negative variances appear. This requires the employee to be proactive in customer needs so that potentially negative situations can be anticipated and dealt with before they become a problem. The focus is excellence in customer service in order to maintain acceptable levels of customer satisfaction.

· Plans, organizes, directs, and controls the operations of company owned or leased motorized vehicles. This activity includes ensuring that regular maintenance of the vehicles is performed in a timely and cost effective manner; monitoring fuel consumption of all vehicles; scheduling repairs resulting from accidents, and developing related accident policies for the driver to follow; and arranging daily car and shuttle schedules for employees and visitors.

· Participates in preparing motorized vehicle lease or purchase and parts agreements with suppliers. This requires this employee to negotiate terms, conditions, and prices which are favorable to the company. He/she will also enter into negotiations with other suppliers for services such as car washes, small repairs, and safety inspections. The critical focus is to provide motorized vehicles that are reliable, safe, and economical to operate.

· Ensures that all the drivers are fully trained in safe driving techniques not only to ensure the safety of his/her passengers but also to promote the image of the company and protect company assets.

POSITION TITLE: Executive Assistant REPORTS TO: Vice President STAFF REPORTS: None

DEPARTMENT: All Departments DATE: May 22nd 1998

PURPOSE OF POSITION: Performs advanced administration and coordination duties of a responsible and confidential
nature.

QUALIFICATIONS: Post Secondary School diploma in Administration or Office Management or equivalent. Three years of office support experience. Aptitude for detailed work and high proficiency in Microsoft Office is required.

SPECIFIC RESPONSIBILITIES: · Provides full administrative support for the VP Team and back up support for the
department. The employee is responsible for word processing, formatting and drafting correspondence, reports, presentations and other material. Material for reports requires the preparation of graphs, charts, and numerical tables from data which is either supplied or must be obtained from searching files and records.

· Arranges and schedules appointments, meetings and conferences for the VP and department, preparing briefing notes for use at/and after meetings; makes travel arrangements and processes the VP's expense reports.

· Monitors the department's monthly budget by reconciling expenditures, reporting positive and negative variances to the VP, and explaining negative variances. The employee also assists in the preparation of the annual department budget for presidential review.

· Answers internal and external telephone calls and receives guests. The employee determines the purpose of the call or visit and handles less critical issues. From time to time he/she is expected to screen potentially unpleasant confrontations. Unresolved matters are forwarded to the VP or Director.

· Uses office equipment such as the fax machine, a personal computer, the photocopier and a calculator. The employee also maintains office supplies, placing orders for supplies and verifying that the supplies are delivered.

· Working conditions are clean and climate controlled in an open office area. There can be exposure to unpleasant situations. Interruptions are frequent and deadlines are somewhat critical.

Summary Knowledge is the factor which reflects how much one needs to know to do the full job competently and satisfactorily. This factor does not measure how much knowledge one has. It evaluates what type of knowledge one must have to perform the position's responsibilities, Key Result Areas (KRAs), and objectives.
Basic knowledge refers to the preparation, education, on-the-job-training and work experience required in order to qualify for the position.
The combination of education and work experience can be a way to gain knowledge. Basic knowledge refers to all knowledge, however obtained. Generally, knowledge obtained in an academic environment can be gained much more rapidly than knowledge that must be learned on the job. Therefore, because knowledge obtained on the job must be learned gradually, over time, substantial work experience will be required to justify equating it to education.
Work experience measures the minimum length of time required to be eligible for the position and to be capable of assuming the position's responsibilities. Over time, the employee should be capable of attaining satisfactory competency levels of quality and quantity. Consideration is given to the amount of time required to perform the position competently, on the basis of continuous progress, rather than on lapsed time.
When rating knowledge, it is important to consider both the thoroughness and the variety required to do the position. Is the position a "deep" specialist with a narrow range of scope (knows a lot about a narrow field) or is the position a generalist with a wide range of different areas (knows a little, but about a lot of different areas).

Summary This factor refers to the type of thinking necessary to solve the problems encountered in performing the major responsibilities of the position. It measures the level of difficulty in using analytical and judgment skills that involve identifying, searching and selecting the best available action in resolving problems.
Positions with broad, unlimited objectives will be evaluated higher than positions that are controlled by established policies and procedures. Also, positions that have minimal recourses and supervision will be rated higher than positions that can receive guidance from their supervisor or readily available resources to solve difficult problems.
Positions that involve a number of unrelated disciplines, which must be integrated to meet different objectives, will be rated higher than positions that are involved in a single area.
Positions that require a considerable amount of time to identify problems, define a problem-solving approach, research alternative solutions, and develop and document recommendations will be rated higher than positions that can solve problems in less time.
Positions that have no guidance from past practices and who usually work with new and unique situations, will be evaluated higher than positions that address similar problems from day to day or from month to month.
As the rating increases, the thinking or analytical skill becomes more difficult. Although there are the same number of sub-factor levels (7) for Knowledge and Problem Solving, there is not an automatic one-to-one relationship. For example, level "E" in Knowledge will not necessarily correspond to a level "E" in Problem Solving. Generally, a Knowledge rating of "E" will be less than "E" under Problem Solving. However, where the position has a primary problem solving focus or some unique specialized focus the same rating may apply. Caution about automatic cross over should be exercised.

Summary Every position makes decisions in order to meet its specific objectives. This work environment has established procedures and reporting relationships in order for an incumbent to take action or make a decision. Consideration is given to how a position is limited by instructions, rules, procedures, precedents and policies, as well as the degree of freedom, guidance or supervision required to make decisions.
In general, policies describe the "what" of a subject, procedures detail the necessary steps to follow through a policy (how, where, when, by whom), and instructions outline the specifics of how to do something.
The following definitions are guidelines:
Instruction - detailed "how to do" directions, such as the instructions which usually accompany electronic devices. Routine - a daily task or frequently performed task; often done automatically. Procedure - taking specific step by step courses of action. Standard - something established by authority to be used as a reference point or model. Precedent - some past practice that serves as an example to justify a practice to follow in similar situations. Policy - a settled course or philosophy adopted by management to be followed when making decisions.
In the following decision making levels, levels A and B require decisions within well defined rules and detailed instructions. Very little discretion or interpretation is required. Levels C and D require decisions within defined procedures with some interpretation required dependent on past practice. Levels E and F require decisions within established policy, requiring broader interpretation. Level G requires thinking about concepts, prindples and general guidelines with a broad organ izatio na I perspective.

Summary This factor measures the level of influence the position has on contributing to its department, customer or company results. The total department's contribution to company results is influenced by the contributions made by each position. Some positions can have a direct impact on their department, customers or company results while others have an indirect impact. Generally, positior-,s in a support role, which provide or maintain information for those who advise, analyze or recommend to others, usually have less of an impact than those positions which are directly accountable for the fir.al results.

The degree of influence the position can have not only reflects the impact it can have on its department's results, but also the type of impact the position can have so that others may make busir.ess decisions. Both are important for contributing to the company's performance. The former provides the foundation for sound business support while the latter takes on more risk because the accountability for the action is more visible.

Summary Working conditions describes the physical environment in which the work is performed. Especially important are any conditions in the physical environment which the employee may find unpleasant or hazardous. Some aspects of the work environment may negatively affect his or her physical well-being.

In some positions there can be conditions that cause stress. These include dealing with difficult situations (usually external), work act:vities under strict deadlines whether scheduled er not, the frequency of interruptions, and whether extensive overnight travel is required. Each person reacts to stress faciors differently. However, the critical test is to focus on what the typical situation may be in the position.
